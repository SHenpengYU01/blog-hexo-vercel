Introduction

SVD(singular value decomposition) is a mathematical method to process matrix. SVD can help extract the key traits of the number in a matrix. Apparently we can use it to process images because images are expressed as matries in computer science. This article is aimed to figure out why and how the SVD can extract the traits of the matrix. Plus I want to give several examples of its application in computere science.

SVD

Start from one way to factorize matrix

Here is a matrix with rank=1rank=1rank=1:

A=(123123)A=\begin{pmatrix} 1&2&3 \\ 1&2&3 \end{pmatrix} A=(11​22​33​)

It can be written as a column times a row:

A=(123123)=(11)(123)A=\begin{pmatrix} 1&2&3 \\ 1&2&3 \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1&2&3 \end{pmatrix} A=(11​22​33​)=(11​)(1​2​3​)

We can say the matrix is decomposed as a column times a row and more generally, for any matrix, it can be decomposed as the sum of column×rowcolumn \times rowcolumn×row with different coefficients. A coefficient decides the weight of the specific column×rowcolumn \times rowcolumn×row in the whole sum. Here is an example:

A=(a000b0)=a(10)(100)+b(01)(010)A=\begin{pmatrix} a&0&0 \\ 0&b&0 \end{pmatrix}=a\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1&0&0 \end{pmatrix}+b\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 0&1&0 \end{pmatrix} A=(a0​0b​00​)=a(10​)(1​0​0​)+b(01​)(0​1​0​)

It can be formalised as

A=σ1u1v1T+σ2u2v2TA=\sigma_1{u_1}v_1^{T}+\sigma_2u_2v_2^{T} A=σ1​u1​v1T​+σ2​u2​v2T​

Here aaa(σ1\sigma_1σ1​) is the weight of the first column×rowcolumn \times rowcolumn×row on the left and bbb(σ2\sigma_2σ2​) is the only other one. If aaa is much more larger than bbb, we can neglect the second product to approximate AAA as only one column×rowcolumn \times rowcolumn×row with its coefficient. This is useful to store the matrix AAA with less space.

But where is the trait? Let’s get it now:
AAA is by m×nm\times nm×n, and suppose it has been decomposed as follow:

A=σ1u1v1T+σ2u2v2T+⋯+σnunvnT(1)A=\sigma_1{u_1}v_1^{T}+\sigma_2u_2v_2^{T}+\cdots+\sigma_n u_n v_n^{T} \tag{1} A=σ1​u1​v1T​+σ2​u2​v2T​+⋯+σn​un​vnT​(1)

where σ1>σ2>⋯>σn\sigma_1>\sigma_2>\cdots>\sigma_nσ1​>σ2​>⋯>σn​.

We can take in equation (1) in this way:
σi\sigma_iσi​ is the weight, uiu_iui​ is the trait vector and viTv_i^{T}viT​ is the combination vector who gives a matrix AiA_iAi​ of m×nm \times nm×n by uiviTu_i v_i^{T}ui​viT​, whose columns are the multiple of uiu_iui​ with their coefficients in viTv_i^{T}viT​.

SVD form

In matrix notation and more formalised, the former example could be written

A=(a000b0)=(1001)(a000b0)(100010001)A=\begin{pmatrix} a&0&0 \\ 0&b&0 \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} 1&0 \\ 0&1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} a&0&0 \\ 0&b&0 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1&0&0 \\ 0&1&0 \\ 0&0&1 \end{pmatrix} A=(a0​0b​00​)=(10​01​)(a0​0b​00​)​100​010​001​​

In a general form of Am×nA_{m\times n}Am×n​, the SVD wants to write AAA as

A=UΣVT(2)A=U\Sigma V^T \tag{2} A=UΣVT(2)

Um×mU_{m\times m}Um×m​ is called the left singular matrix with left singular vectors as its colomns and Vn×nTV^{T}_{n\times n}Vn×nT​ is the right one. Σm×n\Sigma_{m\times n}Σm×n​ has singular values σi\sigma_iσi​ arranged in descending order in position Σii\Sigma_{ii}Σii​ and zeros in other positions.

Usually, UUU and VVV are orthogonal matrix. Therefore, V−1=VTV^{-1}=V^{T}V−1=VT, and multiply VVV in both side of equation (2) we can get another form of it and it is where equation (2) comes from:

AV=UΣ(3)AV=U\Sigma \tag{3} AV=UΣ(3)

The idea is the same as that of diagonalising a real and symmetric square matrix with orthogonal matrix:

A=QΛQT⇔AQ=QΛA=Q\Lambda Q^T \Leftrightarrow AQ=Q\Lambda A=QΛQT⇔AQ=QΛ

where Λ\LambdaΛ is a diagonal matrix with eigen values of AAA and QQQ is the orthogonal matrix with corresponding orthonormal eigen vectors.

Back to equation (3), rewrite it with specific vectors:

A(v1v2⋯vn)=(u1u2⋯um)(σ10⋯00σ2⋯0⋮⋮⋯0)m×nA\begin{pmatrix} v_1&v_2&\cdots &v_n \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} u_1&u_2&\cdots &u_m \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} \sigma_1&0&\cdots &0 \\ 0 &\sigma_2 &\cdots&0\\ \vdots &\vdots&\cdots&0 \end{pmatrix}_{m\times n} A(v1​​v2​​⋯​vn​​)=(u1​​u2​​⋯​um​​)​σ1​0⋮​0σ2​⋮​⋯⋯⋯​000​​m×n​

Suppose there are σ1\sigma_1σ1​ to σr\sigma_rσr​. Then we have

Avi=σiui,i=1,2,⋯ ,r(4)Av_i=\sigma_i u_i, i=1,2,\cdots,r \tag{4} Avi​=σi​ui​,i=1,2,⋯,r(4)

What we need to do is find these singular values σi\sigma_iσi​ and corresponding sigular vectors viv_ivi​ and uiu_iui​, where uiu_iui​ can be given by viv_ivi​ and σi\sigma_iσi​ with equation (4).

Transpose both sides of equation (2):

AT=VΣTUTA^T=V\Sigma^T U^T AT=VΣTUT

Then multiply with AAA:

AAT=(UΣVT)(VΣTUT)=U(ΣΣT)UT(5)AA^T=(U\Sigma V^T)(V\Sigma^T U^T)=U(\Sigma\Sigma^T)U^T \tag{5} AAT=(UΣVT)(VΣTUT)=U(ΣΣT)UT(5)

(ΣΣT)(\Sigma\Sigma^T)(ΣΣT) is a m×m{m\times m}m×m matrix with σ12,σ22,⋯ ,σr2\sigma_1^2,\sigma_2^2,\cdots,\sigma_r^2σ12​,σ22​,⋯,σr2​ and zeros on (ΣΣT)ii(\Sigma\Sigma^T)_{ii}(ΣΣT)ii​ and zero otherwise. Therefore, σi2(i=1,2,...,r)\sigma_i^2(i=1,2,...,r)σi2​(i=1,2,...,r) are eigenvalues of AATAA^TAAT, with uiu_iui​ being their corresponding eigenvectors.
Similarly, we can also get σi2(i=1,2,...,r)\sigma_i^2(i=1,2,...,r)σi2​(i=1,2,...,r) are eigenvalues of ATAA^TAATA, with viv_ivi​ being their corresponding eigenvectors.

SVD application——PCA

PCA(principal component analysis) is a dimensionality reduction technique used in statistics and machine learning. It transforms a dataset with many correlated variables into a smaller set of uncorrelated variables called principal components, while preserving as much variance as possible.
SVD is an effective way to implement PCA.

A visulized example

Take Am×nA_{m\times n}Am×n​ in this way: A set of nnn samples with mmm variables of measurement.
Here is an example from reference[1] listed last.

Suppose we have nnn different points in a 2-D plane and we want to find out the principal direction of these points, namely a line that will be as close as possible to the points.

First, we center each of the measurement: x,yx,yx,y in this example. Substract xˉ\bar{x}xˉ and yˉ\bar{y}yˉ​ for each xix_ixi​ and yiy_iyi​. We can get a matrix A2×nA_{2\times n}A2×n​ with each of its row having average of 000.

We can draw these centered points in a coordinate. The center of these points is the origin. It helps a lot when we try to find the direction formed by these points.

Now consider doing SVD on AAA:

A=UΣVT=(u1u2)Σ(v1v2⋯vn)TA=U\Sigma V^T=\begin{pmatrix} u_1&u_2 \end{pmatrix}\Sigma \begin{pmatrix} v_1&v_2&\cdots&v_n \end{pmatrix}^T A=UΣVT=(u1​​u2​​)Σ(v1​​v2​​⋯​vn​​)T

The leading sigular vector with bigger sigular value shows the direction in the former scatter graph for the reasons we talk about above.

Least perpendicular squares

Also this direction is the direction with least perpendicular squares.

Namely, the sum of squared distances from the points to the line is a minimum.

Proof: Consider a triangle formed by the origin, the line and the point aia_iai​(a vector with(xi,yi)(x_i,y_i)(xi​,yi​)).
Using Pythagorean theorem:

∣∣ai∣∣2=∣aiTu1∣2+∣aiTu2∣2||a_i||^2=|a_i^Tu_1|^2+|a_i^Tu_2|^2 ∣∣ai​∣∣2=∣aiT​u1​∣2+∣aiT​u2​∣2

The first term on the right is the projection of aia_iai​ on the direction of u1u_1u1​(unit vector), namely the principal direction and the second term is the distance square we want. Sum all the distance squares:

dsum=∑i=1n∣aiTu2∣2=∑i=1n∣∣ai∣∣2−∑i=1n∣aiTu1∣2d_{sum}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}|a_i^Tu_2|^2=\sum_{i=1}^{n}||a_i||^2-\sum_{i=1}^{n}|a_i^Tu_1|^2 dsum​=i=1∑n​∣aiT​u2​∣2=i=1∑n​∣∣ai​∣∣2−i=1∑n​∣aiT​u1​∣2

The first term on the right is a constant for given points and the second term can be written as

∑i=1n∣aiTu1∣2=(a1Tu1a2Tu1⋯anTu1)(a1Tu1a2Tu1⋮anTu1)=u1TAATu1\sum_{i=1}^{n}|a_i^Tu_1|^2=\begin{pmatrix} a_1^Tu_1&a_2^Tu_1\cdots&a_n^Tu_1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} a_1^Tu_1\\ a_2^Tu_1\\ \vdots\\ a_n^Tu_1 \end{pmatrix}=u_1^TAA^Tu_1 i=1∑n​∣aiT​u1​∣2=(a1T​u1​​a2T​u1​⋯​anT​u1​​)​a1T​u1​a2T​u1​⋮anT​u1​​​=u1T​AATu1​

Minimise dsumd_{sum}dsum​ means maximise u1TAATu1u_1^TAA^Tu_1u1T​AATu1​. Of course it arrives it maximum when u1u_1u1​ is the singular vector for the maximum singular value. This cooresponds with the PCA by SVD.
